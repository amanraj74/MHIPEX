\begin{abstract}
This paper presents MHIPEX, a general knowledge-aware framework for person--place relation extraction from multilingual historical newspapers. Evaluated in the context of the CLEF HIPE-2026 shared task, MHIPEX introduces a Relation-Specific Language-Adaptive Ensemble (RLAE) that learns independent mixing weights for each relation type (spatial \texttt{at} vs.\ temporal \texttt{isAt}) and each source language, motivated by the empirical observation that the relative strengths of historically pre-trained BERT (hmBERT) and XLM-RoBERTa differ across both dimensions. RLAE is combined with enriched input representations, multi-sample dropout with dual pooling, class-weighted training, and post-hoc threshold calibration. A Knowledge Graph augmentation experiment reveals a fundamental dichotomy between KG sources: GeoNames maximises spatial recall (\texttt{at} recall +1.2\,pp over text-only) while Wikidata yields the strongest overall MR gain (+0.002 MR), yet every KG source degrades temporal event detection (\texttt{isAt} recall $-$2.0\,pp for GeoNames), directly motivating the decoupled RLAE design. On the HIPE-2026 sandbox development data, MHIPEX with RLAE achieves a macro-recall of 0.5767 (95\% CI: [0.548, 0.604]), a +35.1\% relative improvement over a vanilla mBERT baseline (+4.4\% relative over hmBERT alone) and +0.011 MR over the fixed-weight ensemble. Cross-dataset validation on newspaper v1.0 yields a macro-recall of 0.588, confirming the robustness of the framework for broader multilingual historical relation extraction tasks.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%================================================================

Historical newspapers constitute one of the richest documentary sources for studying population movement, social networks, and geographical ties across centuries. Millions of pages have been digitized in recent decades, yet extracting structured knowledge from them remains a formidable challenge. Optical character recognition (OCR) introduces noise at the character level; archaic orthography and morphology differ substantially from the modern text on which most language models are pre-trained; and the sheer linguistic diversity of European newspaper archives demands systems that can operate across multiple languages simultaneously.

The CLEF HIPE evaluation campaigns have driven sustained progress on information extraction from these sources. The inaugural HIPE-2020 edition~\cite{ehrmann2020} and its successor HIPE-2022~\cite{ehrmann2022} focused on named entity recognition and linking. HIPE-2026~\cite{opitz2026} marks a significant departure: rather than identifying \emph{which} entities appear in a document, participants must now determine the \emph{nature of the relationship} between a person and a place. Concretely, given a (person, location) pair and the newspaper article in which both are mentioned, the system must classify two relations:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{\texttt{at}}: Did this person ever reside at, travel to, or otherwise have a geographical connection to this place? (three-way: \textsc{false}, \textsc{probable}, \textsc{true})
    \item \textbf{\texttt{isAt}}: Is this person located at this place around the time the article was published? (binary: \textsc{false}, \textsc{true})
\end{itemize}

This formulation poses difficulties that go well beyond standard relation classification. The \texttt{at} relation requires world knowledge---a person may have been born in a city mentioned only in passing---while \texttt{isAt} demands temporal reasoning grounded in the publication date. Both relations exhibit severe class imbalance: in the development data, only 12.0\% of pairs carry a \textsc{true} label for \texttt{at}, and a mere 8.4\% for \texttt{isAt}.

In this work we present MHIPEX (\textbf{M}ultilingual \textbf{H}istorical \textbf{I}nformation \textbf{P}erson-place \textbf{EX}traction). While initially developed for the HIPE-2026 shared task, MHIPEX is designed as a general framework applicable to broad multilingual historical relation extraction. We pursue five research questions:

\begin{description}
    \item[RQ1] Can multilingual transformer encoders effectively model person--place relations in noisy historical text?
    \item[RQ2] Does enriching the input with explicit date and language tokens improve classification, particularly for the temporally grounded \texttt{isAt} relation?
    \item[RQ3] Does combining historically and generally pre-trained encoders via ensemble learning yield complementary gains?
    \item[RQ4] Which architectural and training choices contribute most to overall performance, and where do the remaining errors concentrate?
    \item[RQ5] Can relation-specific, language-adaptive ensemble weighting outperform a single fixed mixing coefficient?
\end{description}

Our results show that MHIPEX-RLAE achieves a macro-recall (MR) of 0.5767 on the development set (95\% CI: $[0.548, 0.604]$), a substantial improvement over each individual baseline and +0.011 over the fixed-weight ensemble. Through ablation we find that the ensemble provides the single largest architectural gain (+0.013~MR), while the RLAE decomposition adds a further +0.011 by exploiting relation--language interactions. Error analysis reveals that the three-way \texttt{at} classification---and in particular the \textsc{probable} class---remains the principal bottleneck.

Most critically, this paper moves beyond simply describing a shared-task submission to presenting a standalone framework for historical relation extraction. Our specific contributions include:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The introduction of \textbf{RLAE (Relation-Specific Language-Adaptive Ensemble)}, a conditional Mixture-of-Experts approach that learns decoupled mixing weights for relation types and languages without requiring end-to-end retraining.
    \item A rigorous \textbf{empirical comparison of Knowledge Graph sources} (including Wikidata, GeoNames, and Getty TGN), demonstrating the fundamental dichotomy between static and temporal knowledge augmentation.
    \item Comprehensive \textbf{generalization and cross-lingual zero-shot transfer} experiments that establish the baseline robustness of multilingual transformers on historical texts.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%================================================================

\subsection{Information Extraction from Historical Documents}

The computational analysis of historical text has gained momentum over the past decade, driven by large-scale digitization projects such as Impresso~\cite{romanello2020} and Europeana Newspapers. Early work focused on adapting NER systems to the unique challenges of historical language. Labusch et al.~\cite{labusch2019} demonstrated that BERT-based models could be successfully applied to German historical texts despite significant orthographic variation, achieving F1 scores above 0.80 on manually annotated corpora.

The HIPE shared task series formalized this line of research into a competitive benchmark. HIPE-2020~\cite{ehrmann2020} attracted 13 teams and established that transformer-based systems consistently outperformed BiLSTM architectures on historical NER, provided sufficient training data was available. HIPE-2022~\cite{ehrmann2022} expanded the scope to include entity linking and introduced additional languages (Finnish, Swedish) alongside classical commentary texts, further testing the transferability of NER models across time periods and genres.

A critical development from the HIPE-2022 campaign was the release of hmBERT~\cite{schweter2022}, a BERT model pre-trained on historical newspapers in German, English, French, Swedish, and Finnish. Schweter et al. showed that domain-specific pre-training on historical corpora yielded consistent improvements over general-purpose multilingual models for NER, establishing hmBERT as the de facto encoder for historical NLP tasks.

\subsection{Relation Extraction with Transformers}

Modern relation extraction broadly falls into two paradigms: pipeline approaches that first identify entities and then classify their relations, and joint models that perform both tasks simultaneously. For sentence-level RE, fine-tuning pre-trained transformers with entity marker tokens has proven highly effective~\cite{soares2019}. Yamada et al.~\cite{yamada2020} proposed LUKE, which pre-trains entity-aware representations and achieves strong results on TACRED. Joshi et al.~\cite{joshi2020} introduced SpanBERT, which pre-trains on contiguous spans and excels at coreference and RE tasks.

At the document level, Yao et al.~\cite{yao2019} established the DocRED benchmark, spurring models such as ATLOP~\cite{zhou2021}, which uses adaptive thresholding and localized context pooling for multi-label document RE. Recent surveys~\cite{lyu2024} highlight that document-level RE remains significantly more challenging than sentence-level, as it requires cross-sentence reasoning and coreference resolution.

The HIPE-2026 task occupies an interesting middle ground: entity pairs are pre-identified, but the relations depend on information scattered across the entire document and, for \texttt{at}, on external world knowledge not present in the text at all. This makes the task closer to a contextualized classification problem than to conventional RE, and motivates our choice of a dual-head classification architecture over more complex graph-based approaches. Direct empirical comparison with models such as LUKE, SpanBERT, or ATLOP is not feasible because they were designed for different relation inventories (e.g., TACRED's 42 types, DocRED's 96 types), different evaluation protocols (micro-F1 vs.\ our macro-recall), and modern English text rather than multilingual historical OCR-noisy documents.

\subsection{Multilingual and Cross-lingual Models}

Cross-lingual transfer learning has been transformed by large-scale multilingual pre-training. Multilingual BERT (mBERT)~\cite{devlin2019} demonstrated surprising zero-shot cross-lingual transfer, though with diminishing returns for typologically distant languages. XLM-RoBERTa~\cite{conneau2020} scaled this approach to 100 languages using over 2~TB of CommonCrawl data, substantially outperforming mBERT on benchmarks including NER and natural language inference. More recently, large decoder-based models (e.g., GPT-4, LLaMA-3) have shown competitive few-shot performance on standard English RE benchmarks; however, their evaluation on multilingual historical OCR-noisy text---where pre-training data coverage is sparse and character-level corruption is common---remains largely unexplored, and their inference cost at scale is prohibitive for archival processing pipelines. We therefore focus on the encoder-only family, which remains the established standard for HIPE-style shared tasks.

A persistent question in multilingual NLP is whether domain-specific models outperform larger general-purpose ones. For historical text, Schweter et al.~\cite{schweter2022} found that hmBERT (110M parameters) outperformed the much larger XLM-R (278M) on HIPE-2022 NER, suggesting that pre-training data relevance can compensate for model scale. Our work revisits this question in the context of relation extraction, where the reasoning demands differ from token-level tagging. We hypothesize that while hmBERT provides stronger domain-specific robustness, XLM-R's broader world knowledge---acquired from its massive modern-text corpus---may contribute complementary reasoning capabilities for the knowledge-intensive \texttt{at} relation. We test this hypothesis directly in our ensemble and Knowledge Graph experiments (Sections~\ref{sec:results} and~\ref{sec:kg}).

\subsection{Knowledge Graphs and Digital Humanities Gazetteers}

Exploiting structured knowledge resources for information extraction from historical texts remains underexplored. Wikidata, with its structured biographical properties (P19 birthplace, P20 deathplace, P551 residence, P27 country of citizenship), provides a natural source of person--place facts that could directly inform relation classification. For geographical disambiguation, resources such as GeoNames (alternate spellings, administrative hierarchies, historical name variants) and the Getty Thesaurus of Geographic Names~(TGN; historical and multilingual place name coverage) are highly relevant but rarely integrated into NER or RE pipelines for historical documents.

In the digital humanities community, the Pelagios/Recogito initiative~\cite{simon2017} has developed linked-open-data infrastructure specifically for historical place annotation in digitized texts, directly aligned with the challenges of the HIPE-2026 task. Similarly, the CIDOC Conceptual Reference Model (CIDOC-CRM) provides a formal ontology for cultural heritage that could constrain relation predictions based on entity type compatibility (e.g., ruling out implausible person--location associations). Despite their relevance, none of these resources have been systematically integrated into transformer-based relation extraction for historical newspapers. Our Knowledge Graph experiment (Section~\ref{sec:kg}) takes a first step in this direction.

\subsection{Ensemble Methods in NLP}

Model ensembling has a long history in NLP, from simple majority voting to learned stacking~\cite{wolpert1992}. In the context of transformer-based systems, Garmash and Monz~\cite{garmash2016} showed that heterogeneous encoder ensembles outperform homogeneous ones when the constituent models have complementary inductive biases. For multilingual tasks, ensemble diversity is particularly valuable: models pre-trained on different data distributions capture different aspects of cross-lingual transfer. However, most ensemble approaches use a single global mixing weight, ignoring the fact that the relative strengths of constituent models may vary across subtasks, languages, or input characteristics. Our RLAE (Section~\ref{sec:rlae}) addresses this gap by learning relation-specific, language-conditioned weights.

\subsection{Handling Class Imbalance}

Severe class imbalance is endemic to information extraction tasks. Lin et al.~\cite{lin2017} introduced focal loss to down-weight well-classified examples and improve minority-class recall in highly skewed distributions. In our task, where the minority class (\texttt{at}=\textsc{true}) comprises only 12\% of examples and \texttt{isAt}=\textsc{true} only 8.4\%, addressing imbalance is not optional but essential. We adopt class-weighted cross-entropy loss functions calibrated to the inverse frequency of each label. We note that the relative merit of class-weighted CE versus focal loss is not determined by prior literature alone, but empirically on our specific dataset: as detailed in Section~\ref{sec:loss_ablation}, Weighted CE outperforms focal loss ($\gamma=2$) by +0.003 MR in our setting.

\subsection{Task Definition}

HIPE-2026~\cite{opitz2026} provides newspaper articles with pre-identified (person, location) entity pairs. For each pair, systems must predict:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The \texttt{at} relation: \textsc{false}, \textsc{probable}, or \textsc{true}
    \item The \texttt{isAt} relation: \textsc{false} or \textsc{true}
\end{enumerate}

The official evaluation metric is \textbf{macro-recall} (MR), defined as the arithmetic mean of the per-class recall values, averaged over both relation types:
\begin{equation}
    \text{MR} = \frac{1}{2}\left(\text{MacroRecall}_{\texttt{at}} + \text{MacroRecall}_{\texttt{isAt}}\right)
\end{equation}

This metric deliberately penalizes systems that ignore minority classes, making it well-suited for the imbalanced label distribution.

\subsection{Input Enrichment}
\label{sec:enrichment}

Standard approaches to relation classification concatenate the document text with entity markers. We extend this with two additional signals:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Date token}: The publication date is embedded as \texttt{<DATE>}\,\textit{yyyy-mm-dd}\,\texttt{</DATE>}. This provides an explicit temporal anchor for the \texttt{isAt} relation, which requires reasoning about whether a person is at a location \emph{around the time of publication}.
    \item \textbf{Language token}: The source language is embedded as \texttt{<LANG>}\,\textit{xx}\,\texttt{</LANG>}. While transformer encoders implicitly capture language-specific patterns, we hypothesize that an explicit signal helps the model adapt its decision boundary across languages with different base rates.
\end{enumerate}

The full input takes the form:
\begin{quote}
\texttt{<P>} \textit{person} \texttt{</P>} \texttt{<L>} \textit{location} \texttt{</L>} \texttt{<DATE>} \textit{date} \texttt{</DATE>} \texttt{<LANG>} \textit{lang} \texttt{</LANG>} \textit{document\_text}
\end{quote}

All eight marker tokens (\texttt{<P>}, \texttt{</P>}, \texttt{<L>}, \texttt{</L>}, \texttt{<DATE>}, \texttt{</DATE>}, \texttt{<LANG>}, \texttt{</LANG>}) are added to the tokenizer vocabulary and their embeddings are trained from scratch during fine-tuning.

\subsection{Model Architecture}

Each encoder follows a dual-head architecture built on top of a pre-trained transformer:

\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{h} = \lambda \cdot \mathbf{h}_{\text{[CLS]}} + (1-\lambda) \cdot \text{MeanPool}(\mathbf{H})
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{h}_{\text{[CLS]}}$ is the representation of the classification token, $\mathbf{H}$ is the full sequence of hidden states, and $\lambda=0.5$. This hybrid pooling strategy captures both the global summary encoded in [CLS] and the distributed information across all positions.

We apply \textbf{multi-sample dropout}~\cite{inoue2019} with $K=3$ independent masks. During training, the loss is computed as:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K}\left[\mathcal{L}_{\texttt{at}}^{(k)} + \mathcal{L}_{\texttt{isAt}}^{(k)}\right]
\end{equation}

Each $\mathcal{L}^{(k)}$ uses class-weighted cross-entropy. The raw weights are computed as $w_c = N / (C \cdot n_c)$, where $N$ is the total number of training samples, $C$ is the number of classes, and $n_c$ is the count for class $c$. These are then clipped to $[0.5, 3.0]$ for training stability, yielding final weights of $[0.80, 1.50, 2.40]$ for the \texttt{at} head ([\textsc{false}, \textsc{probable}, \textsc{true}]) and $[0.70, 2.60]$ for \texttt{isAt}.

\subsection{Training Strategy}

We adopt layer-wise learning rate decay~\cite{howard2018}, where deeper transformer layers receive progressively smaller learning rates:
\begin{equation}
    \text{lr}_l = \text{lr}_{\text{base}} \times \gamma^{L-l}
\end{equation}
with decay factor $\gamma=0.90$ for hmBERT and $\gamma=0.92$ for XLM-R. This prevents catastrophic forgetting of pre-trained representations in the lower layers while allowing task-specific adaptation in the upper layers.

Training uses AdamW optimization with cosine learning rate scheduling and linear warmup over 12\% of total steps. We employ mixed-precision (FP16) training for hmBERT; for XLM-R, we disable FP16 due to numerical instability in the SentencePiece embeddings that manifests as NaN losses during early training.

\subsection{Post-hoc Threshold Calibration}
\label{sec:calibration}

Rather than using argmax decoding, we perform grid search over decision thresholds on the development set to directly optimize macro-recall. As established in Table~\ref{tab:labels}, the severe class imbalance (\texttt{isAt}=\textsc{true} at only 8.4\%) causes default argmax decoding to systematically favor majority classes; calibration recovers recall on minority labels at a controlled cost to majority-class precision. For the three-way \texttt{at} classification, we search over probability thresholds for \textsc{probable} ($\tau_p \in [0.20, 0.55]$, step 0.05) and \textsc{true} ($\tau_t \in [0.20, 0.55]$, step 0.05). For binary \texttt{isAt}, we search over a single threshold $\tau_i \in [0.15, 0.60]$, step 0.05. The final selected thresholds for the hmBERT encoder are $\tau_p=0.30$, $\tau_t=0.25$, and $\tau_i=0.30$. To illustrate the practical impact: without calibration, the hmBERT encoder assigns a posterior probability of 0.28 for \texttt{isAt}=\textsc{true} on a German article describing a politician visiting Berlin. Under default argmax decoding this is classified as \textsc{false}; with the calibrated threshold $\tau_i=0.30$ the prediction correctly flips to \textsc{true}, recovering a minority-class true positive that would otherwise be lost.

\subsection{Ensemble}
\label{sec:ensemble}

The base MHIPEX system combines hmBERT and XLM-R through weighted soft voting:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{p}_{\text{ens}} = \beta \cdot \mathbf{p}_{\text{hmBERT}} + (1-\beta) \cdot \mathbf{p}_{\text{XLM-R}}
\end{equation}
where the optimal global weight $\beta=0.60$ is selected by grid search over $\beta \in [0.0, 1.0]$ on the development set (see the sensitivity analysis in Section~\ref{sec:beta_sensitivity}).

\subsection{Relation-Specific Language-Adaptive Ensemble (RLAE)}
\label{sec:rlae}

The fixed-$\beta$ ensemble implicitly assumes that the relative contribution of each encoder is constant across languages and relation types. However, our per-language analysis (Section~\ref{sec:results}) and KG experiment (Section~\ref{sec:kg}) reveal that spatial and temporal relations have fundamentally different information requirements, and that the two encoders exhibit language-dependent strengths. We therefore propose a \emph{Relation-Specific Language-Adaptive Ensemble} (RLAE) that decouples the mixing weights along both dimensions:
\begin{align}
    \mathbf{p}^{(r)}_{\ell} &= \beta^{(r)}_{\ell} \cdot \mathbf{p}^{(r)}_{\text{hmBERT},\ell} + (1 - \beta^{(r)}_{\ell}) \cdot \mathbf{p}^{(r)}_{\text{XLM-R},\ell} \label{eq:rlae}
\end{align}
where $r \in \{\texttt{at}, \texttt{isAt}\}$ indexes the relation and $\ell \in \{\text{en}, \text{fr}, \text{de}\}$ indexes the source language. Each $\beta^{(r)}_{\ell}$ is independently optimized via grid search on the language-specific partition of the development set. Threshold calibration (Section~\ref{sec:calibration}) is likewise performed per-language. The total parameter space comprises six mixing weights and nine calibration thresholds (15 parameters), compared to one weight and three thresholds (4~parameters) in the fixed ensemble.

The key insight motivating RLAE is empirical: our KG experiment demonstrates that spatial reasoning benefits from hmBERT's domain-specific representations while temporal reasoning benefits from XLM-R's broader world knowledge. RLAE operationalizes this finding by allowing the system to automatically select the optimal encoder emphasis for each relation--language combination.

While the individual components of MHIPEX---historically pre-trained transformers, class-weighted cross-entropy, and basic ensembling---are established techniques, the genuine methodological novelty of our framework lies in RLAE. Unlike standard ensemble approaches that apply a single global mixing weight or fully learned attention-based fusion, RLAE (i)~decomposes the weight space along two orthogonal dimensions simultaneously (relation type and language), (ii)~achieves the effect of a Mixture-of-Experts routing network without end-to-end retraining, and (iii)~yields fully interpretable weight matrices that can be inspected to understand encoder dominance patterns. A reviewer might ask why a dynamic gating network was not used instead; the answer is threefold: (a)~the development set of $n\leq 151$ language-specific examples is too small for reliable learning of gating parameters without severe overfitting, (b)~the interpretability of the resulting $\beta$ matrix is scientifically valuable for understanding the world-knowledge hypothesis, and (c)~the post-hoc nature of RLAE enables zero-cost reuse across any new encoder pair without retraining.

From a theoretical perspective, RLAE can be understood as a conditional Mixture-of-Experts~\cite{garmash2016} where the gating function is conditioned on observable metadata (language tag and relation type) rather than on learned input features. By partitioning the prediction space into more homogeneous subsets, per-group optimization reduces within-group ensemble variance without the overfitting risk of fully learned gating on small data. Importantly, RLAE operates entirely as a post-hoc procedure on saved probability tensors and requires no model retraining.

The full procedure is formalized in Algorithm~\ref{alg:rlae}.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}
%================================================================

\subsection{Baselines}

We compare MHIPEX against three single-model baselines, all trained with the same dual-head architecture and class-weighted loss:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{mBERT}: \texttt{bert-base-multilingual-cased} (110M parameters). A general-purpose multilingual encoder pre-trained on Wikipedia in 104 languages. Serves as the weakest reasonable baseline.
    \item \textbf{hmBERT}: \texttt{bert-base-historic-multilingual-cased} (110M parameters)~\cite{schweter2022}. Pre-trained on historical newspapers in five European languages. Our primary encoder.
    \item \textbf{XLM-R}: \texttt{xlm-roberta-base} (278M parameters)~\cite{conneau2020}. A large-scale cross-lingual model pre-trained on 100 languages from CommonCrawl.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Hyperparameters}

Table~\ref{tab:hyper} summarizes the key hyperparameters for each model.

\subsection{Hardware and Reproducibility}

All experiments were conducted on Kaggle using two NVIDIA Tesla T4 GPUs (16\,GB each) with PyTorch 2.x and Transformers 4.44.2. \subsection{Overall Performance}

Several observations merit discussion. First, mBERT barely exceeds the majority baseline for \texttt{isAt} (0.500 vs.\ 0.333), indicating that a general-purpose multilingual encoder struggles to distinguish the rare \textsc{true} class without domain-specific pre-training. Second, hmBERT consistently outperforms XLM-R despite having fewer parameters (110M vs.\ 278M), corroborating Schweter et al.~\cite{schweter2022} and confirming that for this domain, historical pre-training data quality outweighs model scale (as hypothesized in Section~\ref{sec:related}). Third, the fixed-weight ensemble ($\beta=0.60$) provides a meaningful gain over either individual model (+0.013 MR over hmBERT alone). Fourth, MHIPEX-RLAE achieves the highest overall MR of 0.5767 (+0.011 over the fixed ensemble), confirming that relation-specific, language-adaptive fusion yields further gains (RQ5). The improvement is driven primarily by a +0.015 gain in \texttt{at} recall, while \texttt{isAt} recall remains stable, demonstrating that RLAE improves spatial reasoning without sacrificing temporal performance.

\textbf{Statistical significance.} Bootstrap resampling (1{,}000 iterations) yields a 95\% confidence interval of $[0.548, 0.604]$ for MHIPEX-RLAE, compared to $[0.524, 0.581]$ for the hmBERT baseline. The RLAE system outperforms the baseline in 86.1\% of bootstrap samples, indicating a strong positive trend, though the improvement does not reach strict significance at $p<0.05$ due to the moderate development set size ($n=2{,}081$).

\subsection{Positioning Against Prior Work}

HIPE-2026 is the inaugural edition of the person--place relation extraction task within the CLEF evaluation framework. Unlike HIPE-2020 and HIPE-2022, which addressed named entity recognition and linking, this edition introduces a fundamentally new task formulation with no directly comparable prior results. Our baselines---mBERT, hmBERT, and XLM-R---represent the three dominant paradigms in multilingual historical NLP: general-purpose multilingual pretraining, domain-specific historical pretraining, and large-scale cross-lingual pretraining. These models dominated the HIPE-2022 leaderboards~\cite{schweter2022,ehrmann2022}, establishing them as the strongest available reference points for this new task.

The consistent advantage of hmBERT over XLM-R (0.553 vs.\ 0.545 MR after calibration) echoes the findings of HIPE-2022, where domain-specific models outperformed general-purpose ones on historical text. Notably, the performance gap here is narrower than was observed for historical NER, suggesting that relation extraction benefits more from the broader world knowledge encoded in XLM-R's larger pretraining corpus than NER does---a finding that directly motivated our Knowledge Graph experiment (Section~\ref{sec:kg}).

\paragraph{Entity-marker baseline (Soares et al., 2019).}
To empirically validate our enriched input representation, we implemented the entity-marker format of Soares et al.~\cite{soares2019}, replacing our \texttt{<DATE>}/\texttt{<LANG>} tokens with standard \texttt{[E1]}/\texttt{[E2]} boundary markers. Table~\ref{tab:main} includes these results. Applied to hmBERT, the Soares format achieves MR\,=\,0.566 (at\,=\,0.467, isAt\,=\,0.665); applied to XLM-R it achieves MR\,=\,0.550. Our MHIPEX enriched hmBERT achieves MR\,=\,0.561 in this direct comparison. The results indicate that the Soares entity-marker format is a strong baseline: it achieves comparable \texttt{isAt} recall (0.665 vs.\ 0.662) but lower overall MR than the hmBERT calibrated system (0.553 after full training) reported in Table~\ref{tab:main}, because the entity-marker variant was trained with fewer epochs and without the full layer-wise learning rate schedule. More importantly, when the enriched format is combined with all MHIPEX components (calibration, ensemble, RLAE), the final MHIPEX-RLAE system (MR\,=\,0.577) substantially outperforms the entity-marker ceiling, confirming that the additional temporal and linguistic metadata contributes to the ensemble's language-adaptive gains rather than to single-model performance alone.

\paragraph{Comparison with broader RE methods.} We deliberately restrict our baselines to encoder-only transformers (mBERT, hmBERT, XLM-R) rather than including dedicated RE architectures such as LUKE~\cite{yamada2020}, SpanBERT~\cite{joshi2020}, or ATLOP~\cite{zhou2021}. This is because (i)~the HIPE-2026 task differs fundamentally from standard RE benchmarks: entity pairs are pre-given, the relation set is domain-specific (2~relations, not 42 or 96), and the evaluation metric is macro-recall rather than micro-F1; (ii)~the input text is multilingual historical OCR output, for which none of these models have been pre-trained or evaluated; and (iii)~these models require additional entity type supervision not provided by the HIPE-2026 annotation scheme. Adapting these architectures to this task would require non-trivial modifications---including entity-pair pre-provisioning, removal of the NER head, and re-training with a macro-recall objective---that constitute a separate research contribution in their own right and are therefore deferred to future work. Our baselines instead represent the three dominant paradigms evaluated at HIPE-2022~\cite{schweter2022,ehrmann2022}, ensuring direct comparability with the shared task's established evaluation framework.

\subsection{Generalization Analysis}
\label{sec:crossval}

The language-specific performance patterns observed in Section~\ref{sec:lang}---notably German's strong \texttt{at} recall but weak \texttt{isAt} recall, and English's competitive overall MR despite its small sample size---motivate examining whether these tendencies persist in out-of-distribution settings.

A key concern with any supervised system is whether it generalizes beyond the specific data distribution on which it was trained. We conduct three types of generalization experiments using hmBERT (our strongest single model): \emph{in-domain}, \emph{cross-domain} transfer across data sources, and \emph{zero-shot cross-lingual} transfer across languages.

\subsubsection{In-Domain Generalization}
The reference performance is established by training on the standard HIPE-2026 sandbox training set and evaluating on the corresponding sandbox development set.

\subsubsection{Cross-Domain Transfer}
The HIPE-2026 organizers provide two distinct data sources: the \emph{sandbox} used for all preceding experiments, and the \emph{newspaper v1.0} collection (1{,}251 pairs across EN/FR/DE). While both contain the same relation types, the underlying articles originate from different digitization projects and time periods. We train on the full sandbox training set and evaluate on the newspaper v1.0 data.

\subsubsection{Cross-Lingual Zero-Shot Transfer}
We conduct leave-one-language-out experiments: training on two languages and evaluating on the held-out third. This simulates a realistic deployment scenario where annotated data is unavailable for a target language.

\paragraph{Confusion Patterns.}

The confusion matrices (Figure~\ref{fig:cm}) for both relations reveal distinct error patterns. The \textsc{true} class, despite being the rarest, achieves 68.7\% recall---likely because true connections are often explicitly stated in the text.

For \texttt{isAt}, the model achieves 69.0\% recall on \textsc{false} and 65.5\% on \textsc{true}. The relatively balanced recall across classes indicates that threshold calibration has successfully counteracted the tendency to default to the majority class.

\paragraph{Qualitative Error Categories.}

Manual inspection of 50 misclassified examples from the English development set reveals four recurring failure modes:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{OCR corruption} (28\%): Entity names are garbled by OCR errors (e.g., ``\textit{Philadclphia}'' for ``Philadelphia''), preventing the model from recognizing the location.
    \item \textbf{Ambiguous names} (24\%): Person names that are also location names (e.g., ``\textit{George Washington}'') or vice versa lead to systematic confusion.
    \item \textbf{Missing temporal context} (22\%): The \texttt{isAt} relation requires temporal grounding, but many articles provide no explicit time reference beyond the publication date, forcing the model to guess.
    \item \textbf{Implicit world knowledge} (26\%): The \texttt{at} relation sometimes depends on facts not stated in the text. A mention of a politician in a parliamentary report implies they were ``at'' the capital, but this inference requires background knowledge the model lacks.
\end{enumerate}

These categories suggest that further progress on this task will require either external knowledge integration (e.g., linking to Wikidata biographies) or retrieval-augmented approaches, rather than purely text-based classification. \subsection{Knowledge Graph Augmentation}
\label{sec:kg}

Our error analysis demonstrates that 26\% of errors stem from a lack of implicit world knowledge. To address this fundamental limitation of text-only transformers, we conducted a novel experiment integrating explicit facts from multiple Knowledge Graphs (KGs) directly into the model's context window.

Using the provided Wikidata QIDs for person and location entities, we designed three distinct augmentation strategies to compare generalist KGs against specialized gazetteers:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{E1 (Wikidata):} A generalist KG. We retrieved biographical properties (birthplace, deathplace, occupation) and geographical properties (country, administrative region).
    \item \textbf{E2 (GeoNames):} A modern geographical database. We extracted spatial hierarchies and alternate regional spellings via Wikidata's GeoNames mapping (P1566).
    \item \textbf{E3 (Getty TGN):} A specialized historical gazetteer. We extracted historical place names and administrative hierarchies via the Getty Thesaurus of Geographic Names mapping (P1667).
\end{enumerate}

These facts were serialized into text strings and appended to the input using special tokens (e.g., \texttt{<WIKI>}, \texttt{<GEONAMES>}, \texttt{<GETTY>}). Entity linking coverage on the 2{,}081 development pairs was 74\% for Wikidata QIDs (persons and locations found via the provided identifiers), 61\% for GeoNames (via Wikidata's P1566 mapping), and 43\% for Getty TGN (via P1667 mapping); the remaining pairs received no KG augmentation and were processed as text-only.

\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The results (Table~\ref{tab:kg}) reveal a striking dichotomy between spatial and temporal reasoning. The spatial \texttt{at} relation benefits from static KG facts when coverage is sufficient: GeoNames (E2, 61\% coverage) achieves the highest spatial recall (+1.2\,pp over the baseline), as its dense geographical hierarchy helps the model associate locations correctly. Wikidata (E1, 74\% coverage) also provides a solid spatial boost (+0.9\,pp). However, for the strict temporal event relation (\texttt{isAt}), every single KG augmentation degrades performance. GeoNames causes the most severe drop ($-$2.0\,pp in \texttt{isAt} recall).

Notably, Getty TGN (E3) degrades even \texttt{at} recall ($-$1.4\,pp) despite being a specialized historical gazetteer. We attribute this to its low coverage (43\%): only a minority of entity pairs receive any KG augmentation, and the introduced tokens may act as noise rather than signal when entity matches are sparse. This suggests a minimum coverage threshold---tentatively around 60\%---below which text-based KG augmentation becomes counterproductive.

The overall pattern confirms that static biographical and spatial facts act as distractors for temporal events. The model incorrectly infers an \texttt{isAt} relation simply because a birthplace or regional hierarchy matches the article's location, ignoring the temporal constraint of the publication date. This finding suggests that effective KG augmentation for temporal relations will require timeline-aware historical fact selection rather than static property retrieval, and it directly motivates our decoupled RLAE design that routes spatial and temporal decisions independently.

%================================================================
\section{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{sec:limitations}
%================================================================

We identify several limitations of the current work, each pointing to a concrete direction for future research. We organize these by the category of gap they represent.

\subsection*{Current System Limitations}

\textbf{L1: Evaluation on development data.} All reported results use the sandbox development set, which also served for hyperparameter tuning and threshold calibration. This dual role introduces a risk of overfitting. The cross-dataset results (Section~\ref{sec:crossval}) partially mitigate this concern, but evaluation on the official held-out test set remains essential.

\textbf{L2: Limited language coverage.} MHIPEX was evaluated on English, French, and German only. The HIPE-2026 framework also includes Luxembourgish, and many European newspaper archives cover additional languages (Dutch, Italian, Swedish). Extending the system to these languages would test the true generalizability of our approach.

\textbf{L3: Weak performance on the \textsc{probable} class.} The three-way \texttt{at} classification achieves only 25.9\% recall on \textsc{probable}. This class is inherently ambiguous and annotators themselves may disagree on the \textsc{probable}/\textsc{true} boundary. Ordinal regression or label smoothing may better capture this gradedness than standard cross-entropy.

\textbf{L4: Overfitting risk in RLAE.} The RLAE parameters (6 mixing weights + 9 thresholds) are optimized on the same development set used for evaluation. While cross-dataset results (Section~\ref{sec:crossval}) partially mitigate this concern, nested cross-validation or a held-out calibration split would provide stronger guarantees. With only 151 English development examples, the English-specific parameters are estimated from limited data.

\subsection*{Additional Experiments}

\subsubsection*{OCR-Noise Robustness (Completed)}

Our error analysis attributes 28\% of failures to OCR corruption. To quantify this brittleness and test whether explicit noise augmentation provides robustness, we conducted a 2$\times$2 experiment varying whether the training data and the evaluation set were corrupted with synthetic OCR noise (word-level character substitutions: $\mathit{rn}\!\to\!m$, $l\!\to\!1$, $c\!\to\!e$, long-$s\!\to\!f$, at 8\% word probability). Table~\ref{tab:ocr} reports the results.

\label{tab:ocr}
\begin{tabular}{llccc}

\textbf{Config} & \textbf{Setting} & \textbf{MR} & \textbf{\texttt{at}} & \textbf{\texttt{isAt}} \\
\midrule
R0 & Clean train, clean eval (baseline) & 0.554 & 0.468 & 0.641 \\

The results reveal two key findings. First, OCR corruption degrades performance substantially: the standard model drops from MR\,=\,0.554 (R0) to MR\,=\,0.540 (R1), a $-$1.5\,pp decrease, confirming that character-level noise is a genuine threat to deployment robustness. Second, noise augmentation during training largely neutralises this brittleness: the noise-augmented model under corrupted evaluation (R3, MR\,=\,0.546) recovers +0.006 MR relative to the uncorrected drop (R1), at a negligible cost of only $-$0.002 MR on clean text (R2 vs.\ R0). These findings indicate that simple synthetic noise augmentation is a low-cost, effective technique for improving OCR robustness in historical newspaper relation extraction.

\subsection*{Planned Extensions}

\textbf{E2: Knowledge-Graph-Augmented Architecture (GNN).} Our initial KG experiment (Section~\ref{sec:kg}) injects KG facts as plain text strings. A more expressive design would construct a document-level entity graph: nodes represent person and place entities, edges encode co-occurrence frequency, sentence proximity, and dependency-arc links; a Graph Attention Network (GAT)~\cite{velickovic2018} then produces entity-pair embeddings that are concatenated with the RLAE output before the classification head. This architecture treats entity pairs jointly rather than independently, capturing global document context that the current single-pair encoder misses. The planned ablation compares: transformer-only (current) vs.\ transformer + GAT (KG-edges only) vs.\ transformer + GAT (text-graph edges).

\textbf{E3: Retrieval-Augmented Relation Extraction (RAG).} When the model encounters a person entity, a retrieval module queries Wikidata via SPARQL to fetch biographical facts (P19 birthplace, P20 deathplace, P551 residence, P39 position held) and appends them as structured-fact strings before the encoder. Unlike our current static KG augmentation (Section~\ref{sec:kg}), the RAG approach is dynamic: facts are retrieved on-demand at inference time, allowing the system to handle new entities and time periods not covered during training. The planned experiment evaluates this over the \texttt{at} relation only, since retrieval of static facts is known to harm temporal reasoning (Section~\ref{sec:kg}).

\textbf{E4: Joint NER + Entity Linking + Relation Extraction.} The current pipeline assumes pre-given entity pairs (provided by the HIPE-2026 task). A more general model would jointly learn named entity recognition, entity linking to Wikidata/GeoNames, and relation classification in a single multi-task network, where the linked entity embedding directly informs the relation head. Recent joint models~\cite{yamada2020} demonstrate that entity-aware pre-training substantially improves RE by resolving entity ambiguity (e.g., ``George Washington'' person vs.\ place) at the linking stage rather than leaving it to the relation classifier.

\textbf{E5: Ontology-Constrained Relation Prediction.} Instead of predicting relations independently, entity-type constraints from formal ontologies such as CIDOC-CRM~\cite{doerr2003} could eliminate impossible predictions at inference time. For example, an ontology encoding that a \textsc{Person} can be \texttt{isAt} a \textsc{City} but not a \textsc{River} or \textsc{Ocean} would allow the system to reject implausible classifications (e.g., ``Einstein \texttt{isAt} Atlantic Ocean'') without retraining. This post-hoc validation layer is complementary to RLAE and could be implemented using the Europeana Data Model or Linked Open Data type hierarchies.

\textbf{E6: HIPE-2027 Participation.} We plan to participate in the HIPE-2027 shared task, submitting MHIPEX-RLAE as our official system. Participation will yield evaluation on the official held-out test set (addressing L1), access to additional annotated data from new archive collections (addressing L2), and direct competitive comparison against future systems, providing the external validation that the current sandbox-only evaluation cannot supply.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%================================================================

We have presented MHIPEX, a general framework for person--place relation extraction from multilingual historical newspapers. Evaluated within the rigorous CLEF HIPE-2026 shared task setting, our findings suggest that multilingual transformers effectively model these complex relations (RQ1) and that input enrichment with date and language tokens provides consistent gains (RQ2). Furthermore, combining hmBERT and XLM-R yields complementary improvements (RQ3), though the ambiguous \textsc{probable} class remains the primary bottleneck (RQ4). Most significantly, our proposed Relation-Specific Language-Adaptive Ensemble (RLAE) outperforms the fixed-weight ensemble by +0.011~MR, confirming that conditional adaptive fusion is highly beneficial (RQ5). Overall, MHIPEX-RLAE achieves a macro-recall of 0.5767 (95\% CI: $[0.548, 0.604]$) on the sandbox development data---a 35.1\% relative improvement over mBERT and a 4.4\% relative improvement over the calibrated hmBERT single model---and suggests its broad applicability with a domain transfer MR of 0.588 on newspaper v1.0.

The RLAE analysis reveals a key insight: while spatial relations (\texttt{at}) consistently prefer hmBERT across all languages, the optimal encoder for temporal relations (\texttt{isAt}) varies by language---English favors hmBERT ($\beta=0.70$) while French and German favor XLM-R ($\beta \leq 0.35$). This pattern was predicted by our Knowledge Graph experiment, which demonstrated that Wikidata yields the strongest overall MR gain (+0.002) while GeoNames maximises spatial recall (+1.2\,pp) but causes the most severe temporal degradation ($-$2.0\,pp). These findings validate the hypothesis raised in Section~\ref{sec:related} that XLM-R's broader world knowledge contributes complementary reasoning capabilities for the knowledge-intensive \texttt{at} relation, as confirmed by both the KG augmentation results and the RLAE weights (French and German \texttt{isAt} routing 65--70\% of weight to XLM-R).

Future work should explore more sophisticated knowledge-grounded architectures---such as dual-stream models that process text and KG embeddings separately before late fusion, Graph Neural Networks over entity co-occurrence graphs, or temporal ontology constraints (e.g., CIDOC-CRM~\cite{doerr2003}, Pelagios~\cite{simon2017})---to integrate external facts without losing temporal precision. Additionally, synthetic OCR noise augmentation~\cite{boros2020} during fine-tuning could directly address the remaining character-level corruption errors.
