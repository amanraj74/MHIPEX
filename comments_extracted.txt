1. Aim/Objective & Contributions ΓÇö 3/5 
The methodological novelty is incremental rather than algorithmic. The main contribution 
is the engineering combination of existing techniques (hmBERT, XLM-R, enriched inputs, 
class-weighted loss, calibration, and soft-voting ensemble) for the HIPE-2026 shared task. The 
paper should explicitly clarify what is genuinely novel beyond the integration of known 
components.

Found concrete, unresolved arithmetic inconsistencies in Table 3. Since MR is defined as the 
average of MR_at and MR_isAt, the reported values in Table 3 should satisfy this relationship 
exactly (up to rounding). Several rows do not, suggesting transcription or reporting errors rather 
than numerical rounding. These inconsistencies should be corrected before publication. : 
ΓùÅ hmBERT (Single): MR = 0.546, MR_at = 0.450, MR_isAt = 0.655 ΓåÆ average = 
(0.450+0.655)/2 = 0.5525, not 0.546. 
ΓùÅ hmBERTΓÇá (+Calibr.): MR = 0.553, with the same MR_at = 0.450 and MR_isAt = 0.655 as 
the uncalibrated row. By the paper's own metric definition (Eq. 1), MR must equal the 
average of MR_at and MR_isAt ΓÇö it cannot change from 0.546 to 0.553 while both

ΓùÅ XLM-R (Single): MR = 0.538, MR_at = 0.447, MR_isAt = 0.643 ΓåÆ average = 0.545, not

Structure, Flow, and Section Headings 
1. Limitations should be a separate, labeled subsection, not folded into the final 
paragraph of the Conclusion. Currently the dev-set leakage caveat and the 3-language 
caveat are buried as a single paragraph after the summary ΓÇö pulling these into a 
"Limitations" subsection (common best practice, e.g., ACL-style papers) would make 
them more visible to reviewers and readers, and is more appropriate than having them 
appear only at the very end after future work is already discussed. 
 
2. Section 5.2's per-language results (right half of Table 4) sit awkwardly alongside 
the ablation study (left half). These are two different analyses sharing one 
table/section. Consider moving the per-language results out of 5.2 and merging them 
with Section 5.3 (Cross-Dataset and Cross-Lingual Validation), since both address 
language-level generalization ΓÇö this would create a cleaner "Generalization" section 
(in-domain per-language performance + cross-dataset transfer + zero-shot transfer) 
separate from the pure "Ablation" section.

already established in Sec. 3.1/Table 1 would tighten this rather than relying on the 
reader to recall those numbers from two pages earlier.

2: OCR-Noise-Aware Contrastive Robustness Training 
Motivation: 28% of inspected errors are attributed to OCR corruption (their example: "Philadclphia" for 
"Philadelphia"). The current pipeline does nothing explicit to address this ΓÇö it relies entirely on 
hmBERT's historical pre-training to absorb OCR noise implicitly. 
Proposed experiment: Synthetically inject realistic OCR-style character confusions (common 
substitutions like rnΓåöm, lΓåö1, cΓåöe, long-sΓåöf, digit/letter swaps) into training data, then train with a 
contrastive or consistency objective that pulls clean and OCR-corrupted versions of the same entity 
mention together in representation space. Ablate against the current model to see whether this 
measurably improves recall specifically on the OCR-corruption-flagged subset of their own 50-example 
error analysis (which they could re-annotate as a small diagnostic test set ΓÇö itself a methodological 
contribution, since right now that 50-example analysis is descriptive only, not used as an evaluation set). 
 
The paper itself identifies that many errors require external world knowledge: 
ΓùÅ George Washington (person vs place) 
ΓùÅ politicians and capitals 
ΓùÅ historical place names 
ΓùÅ OCR-induced ambiguity 
Currently the model only uses text.
